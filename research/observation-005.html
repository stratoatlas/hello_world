Observation 005 Research log

The gap
between models

Today’s AI infrastructure is built primarily around individual models, not between them. This creates a structural gap — no stable infrastructure exists between models yet. The gap is visible. The position is empty.

Roman Kir
StratoAtlas Research
March 2026
Working hypothesis

When the StratoAtlas pipeline began using one model for extraction and another for dialogue, this looked like an inconsistency. It is not. It is the first deliberate step into a space that the industry is treating as a problem to be solved — rather than a position to be occupied.

01 — The structural situation

Everyone is building inside. Nobody is building between.

The current landscape of AI infrastructure has a clear geometry. Each laboratory — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, Mistral — primarily builds tooling, APIs, agent frameworks, and evaluation infrastructure optimised around its own models and their internal logic. This is rational. Vertical integration produces coherence, speed, and competitive differentiation.

It also produces a blind spot.

Each laboratory primarily optimises inward. The space between models has no established infrastructure, no vocabulary, and no recognised research position. Users already live there. Tooling does not yet exist.

The blind spot is not technical. It is conceptual. The inter-model space is treated as a compatibility problem — something to be bridged, translated, or standardised away. The assumption is that the right outcome is a world where models are interchangeable, and the seams between them are invisible.

That assumption may be wrong. And if it is wrong, it is wrong in an interesting direction.

02 — The user's reality

Users are already multi-model. Tools are not.

The standard framing: a user chooses a model. They evaluate options, pick the best one for their use case, and commit. The model is a tool. The goal is to find the right tool.

A pattern increasingly visible in practice in 2024–2025: users switch between models constantly — not because they are undecided, but because different models are genuinely better at different things. A user might draft with Claude, code with GPT-4o, research with Gemini, and run structured extraction with a fine-tuned smaller model. This is not confusion. It is a rational response to a heterogeneous landscape.

The gap between how AI laboratories think about model choice (pick one, commit) and how users actually behave (use several, switch fluidly) is itself a structural tension. In CDSA terms: a productive contradiction, not a problem to resolve.

The infrastructure that exists assumes the first pattern. The behaviour that is emerging follows the second. No stable tooling has been built for the second pattern yet — not because it is technically hard, but because it requires accepting that models are not interchangeable, and that the differences between them are a feature rather than a defect.

03 — The conceptual move

Models as positions, not tools

A tool is interchangeable with another tool of equal quality. If two hammers drive nails equally well, it does not matter which you use. The goal is to find the best hammer.

A position is not interchangeable. Two people can observe the same event from different positions and produce genuinely different, both valid, descriptions. The difference between their descriptions is not error — it is information about the structure of the event itself.

Different AI models are trained on different data distributions, with different alignment procedures, different architectural decisions, and different implicit assumptions about what constitutes a good response. When two models give different answers to the same question, that difference is not noise to be filtered. It is signal about the question.

Central thesis

Models are not interchangeable tools for producing outputs. They are positions — each with a characteristic way of structuring problems, weighting evidence, and resolving ambiguity.

The gap between two models on the same input is not a compatibility problem. It is the same structural signal as the gap between two AIs in a trialogue: an indicator of where the subject is genuinely uncertain, where the framing is contested, where the knowledge is model-specific rather than universal.

This is the conceptual move that changes what inter-model divergence means. Not: which model is right? But: what does their disagreement reveal about the subject?

04 — The calibration instrument

Disagreement as data

In the StratoAtlas pipeline, CDSA extraction currently runs through one model. The trialogue configuration uses another. This was not planned — it emerged from the sequence of decisions made as the system was built.

Looking at it now, the accidental architecture reveals something useful. When the same forum thread is classified by two different models, there are three possible outcomes:

Agreement (≡) signals robust classification — the case is clearly within a category.
Disagreement (≠) signals one of three things: the case is genuinely borderline, the category definition is underspecified, or the prompt is coupling to model-specific behaviour rather than the framework's own concepts.

High agreement rate across a category may indicate robust classification — the framework capturing something real, not something model-specific. Or it may indicate shared model artifacts: both models trained on similar data, producing similar blind spots. High disagreement rate is the clearer signal: it makes definitional weakness visible fastest.

The prompt as the instrument

The processor prompt is the executable operationalization of the CDSA framework. If it is written in the framework's own concepts — not in terms of what a specific model responds well to — it should produce consistent classification across models. Inconsistency is a signal that the prompt is doing model-specific work it should not be doing.

Prompt-model coupling is a form of definitional imprecision. Inter-model disagreement makes it visible.

This is why the processor prompt must be versioned and documented as a research artifact. When it changes, the classifications change. When it is model-specific, the classifications are not about the framework — they are about the model.

This is what makes inter-model calibration more than a quality check. When CDSA uses divergence between models to stress-test its own classification categories, the framework is no longer just a tool for diagnosing external contradictions. It becomes a framework capable of stress-testing its own definitional precision. The same method applied outward — to forum cases — and inward — to its own category boundaries — simultaneously.

05 — The connection to trialogue

The same structure at two scales

Observation 003 described the gap between two AIs in a trialogue as the primary instrument — not an expansion of context, but a mechanism that makes the frame visible. Where two AIs diverge, that divergence marks the live boundary of confident knowledge.

Inter-model divergence in a research pipeline is the same structure at a different scale. The trialogue produces gaps in real time, in a single session, for a specific question. The pipeline produces gaps systematically, across many cases, for a framework's classification categories.

One AI gives an answer — you see the subject.
Two AIs give different answers — you see the contours of the subject.
Two different AI models give different answers — you see where the subject resists model-independent description.

The trialogue uses the gap as an instrument for positional work — helping the human see which frame they are operating from. The pipeline uses the gap as a calibration instrument — helping the researcher see where the framework's definitions are doing insufficient work.

The same principle appears to operate at two scales. Different application, different granularity — but the same structural logic. Whether this homology is deep or coincidental is itself an open question worth tracking.

06 — The position

What StratoAtlas is doing, stated plainly

StratoAtlas is not trying to be model-agnostic in the standard sense — that is, indifferent to which model is used, aiming for interchangeability. It is doing something different: using multiple models deliberately, treating their differences as information, and building a research practice around the gaps between them.

This is a position, not a workaround. It does not require any model to be better than another. It requires accepting that models are different in ways that matter — and that those differences can be made to do research work.

What this is not

It is not a comparison of models to find the best one. It is not a benchmark. It is not a hedge against dependency on a single provider. Those are reasonable goals with different methods.

What this is

A research practice that uses inter-model divergence as a signal — about the subject being studied, about the quality of the framework's definitions, and about where knowledge is model-specific rather than universal. The gaps are the instrument.

The infrastructure for this does not exist yet. No standard tooling for tracking inter-model disagreement on structured classification tasks. No vocabulary for distinguishing productive divergence from simultaneous error. No established practice for using model differences as calibration rather than noise.

That gap — in the infrastructure, in the vocabulary, in the practice — is the position StratoAtlas is moving into. Not because it was planned, but because the work led here.

Working hypothesis, not a demonstrated practice

This observation describes a position that StratoAtlas is beginning to occupy, not one it has fully developed. Inter-model disagreement tracking does not yet exist in the pipeline. The calibration instrument described in section 04 is a design, not an implemented system.

What exists: the observation that the accidental multi-model architecture reveals something useful. What does not yet exist: the tooling to exploit it systematically.

The open questions below are the actual unknowns. Some of them may turn out to make this observation premature.

Conclusion

The standard framing treats inter-model differences as a compatibility problem — something to be standardised away so that models become interchangeable. This framing produces a certain kind of infrastructure: translation layers, benchmark comparisons, provider-agnostic APIs.

An alternative framing: models are positions, and their differences are information. This produces a different kind of infrastructure — one that tracks divergence, uses disagreement as calibration, and treats the gaps between models as the primary instrument.

The second kind has no established infrastructure, vocabulary, or recognised practice yet. The position is structurally visible — and empty. The work that led StratoAtlas here was not planned — it emerged from building a pipeline that happened to use two different models for two different tasks.

The accidental architecture may turn out to be the right architecture. Not because it was chosen, but because it corresponds more closely to how users already live — in a multi-model world that the infrastructure has not yet caught up with.

Open questions
How do you distinguish productive inter-model divergence — divergence that reveals something about the subject — from simultaneous error in both models? Is an external criterion needed, and if so, what would it be?
If the processor prompt is written to be model-agnostic, will agreement rates actually increase — or will they reveal that the CDSA categories themselves are model-sensitive at the boundary cases?
Is there a minimum number of models needed to make inter-model divergence statistically meaningful as a calibration instrument — or is two sufficient for the purpose?
When models agree on a classification, is that evidence of robust classification — or evidence that both models share the same training artifacts, making agreement uninformative?
At what point does inter-model divergence become a research instrument rather than a signal? That is: what conditions must be met for a divergence to do methodological work — rather than simply indicate that something is uncertain?
